The Access Collins alternative for venues where the deck is the product

Access Collins runs UK bar and club bookings well. Clubtech sells the daybed: a spatial map, zone pricing, dayparts, prepaid packages. Here's how to choose.

Book a Demo See the Platform

A London cocktail bar and a Bali beach club both take bookings, but they are not selling the same thing. The bar is managing enquiries and covers against a service period. The beach club is selling a specific $450 front-row daybed, a $2,000 cabana package, and the bottles stacked on top — a physical piece of the deck, at a price that changes by zone and daypart. If you're a beach club, day club, or hotel pool weighing Access Collins, the question isn't whether it takes bookings well. It's whether the deck is your product — and whether an enquiry-and-bookings model can sell it.

What Access Collins does well — an honest summary

Credit first. Access Collins is a real, mature booking system with a track record beach-and-day-club software rarely matches. It was founded in 2011 and acquired by The Access Group in 2017, so it now sits inside a large enterprise software suite alongside POS and workforce tools — the kind of vendor a UK hospitality group's procurement team is comfortable buying from.

Its consumer side is a genuine strength. DesignMyNight is a discovery site with up to 3.9 million monthly users across more than 5,000 UK venues, so a bar or club on the platform can be found by people actively looking for a night out. Collins is widely used by UK bar and club groups to manage enquiries, pre-orders, and bookings, and its newer Collins Evo AI booking system is marketed on a claimed 24% conversion-rate increase. Pricing is public and starts from £149/month per site, scaling by site type, size, and features.

If you run a group of UK late-night bars and clubs, Collins is a serious, defensible choice, and this page won't pretend otherwise. The mismatch starts when your inventory isn't a table for a party at a time — it's furniture on a map.

Beach clubs sell furniture, zones, and dayparts — not enquiries

An enquiry-and-bookings model asks: which party, what date, what time? A beach club guest is answering a different question — which spot is mine?

  • The inventory is physical and unequal. Front row is not third row. Swim-up is not the shade line. A daybed by the DJ booth and a daybed by the walkway are different products at different prices, even though both seat four.
  • The unit of sale is the package, not the seat. Bed only, bed plus minimum spend, party package, ultimate experience — with bottles, cakes, and transfers added at checkout. The upsell happens in the booking flow, not at the door.
  • The day has dayparts, not just a service period. Morning and afternoon sessions on the same furniture, sunset premiums, event-day pricing. Your Saturday isn't one sitting; it's one map sold several ways.

Clubtech was built around that map. Guests open a 3D birds-eye interactive plan of the venue, explore the zones, tap the exact piece of furniture they want, and watch 360° walkthroughs before they commit. By the time the price appears, they aren't evaluating a reservation — they're protecting a spot they've already chosen. That's the difference between a nightclub table booking sold on a map and one taken as an enquiry.

Where an enquiry-and-bookings model doesn't fit the deck

None of this makes Access Collins bad software. It makes it software optimized for a different transaction. Operators who arrive at Clubtech from a bookings-first tool tend to report the same friction:

  1. No guest-facing spatial choice. An enquiry-and-bookings flow can't sell the difference between front row and back row, so the premium zones — your highest-margin inventory — get allocated by staff instead of sold at a premium online.
  2. No zone or daypart pricing as a first-class product. Beach and day clubs need the same "bed type" to cost triple in the front row on a Saturday afternoon. Dynamic pricing by zone, date, and daypart is how the same furniture earns more; booking rules against a service period approximate it at best.
  3. A rented audience, not an owned channel. DesignMyNight discovery traffic is real, but it arrives through the DesignMyNight brand — the venue rents that audience. Ask the ownership question plainly: whose guest is it, and whose remarketing list does the booking build? Clubtech is white-label on your own domain, and the guest account and data stay with the venue.
  4. No native ads-revenue loop. Public Collins materials don't market a server-side pipeline that posts a paid booking and its revenue back to Meta, Google, and GA4. For a beach or day club spending real money on paid social to fill Saturdays, that loop is the difference between optimizing on clicks and optimizing on dollars.

There's a fit question of geography and payments, too. Collins is UK-centric. A beach or day club in the SEA, Med, or Gulf markets needs Midtrans — QRIS, GoPay, OVO — Airwallex, and true multi-currency at checkout, which is where Clubtech's beach club platform is built to run.

The ads and revenue loop, compared

The second gap is what happens around the booking. On Clubtech, the booking is the conversion event, piped to Meta, Google, and GA4 in real time. Lookalike audiences are seeded from your highest-LTV bookers. Abandoned carts fire CAPI events within seconds, so dynamic ads return a guest to the exact zone, date, and price they walked away from. Revenue is posted back for value-based optimization, so your ad platforms learn on dollars, not conversions.

Collins is one module inside a large horizontal vendor, and its consumer demand comes through the DesignMyNight discovery brand rather than a server-side pipeline into your own ad accounts. Both are legitimate ways to reach guests. But they answer different questions. Discovery traffic asks: can new people find the venue? The ads-revenue loop asks: does a paid booking, and its revenue, reach the ad platform automatically, and does an abandoned cart come back as a retargetable event?

Mobile matters here too. On Clubtech, 82% of bookings happen on a phone after 10pm — the guest is planning Saturday from the sofa on Thursday night. Sub-second load, no app install, checkout in four taps, Apple Pay and Google Pay.

Can you run Access Collins and Clubtech together?

For a hospitality group, this often isn't an either/or. The natural split is by venue type. Concede Collins for a UK group's London bars, where enquiry management, pre-orders, and DesignMyNight discovery earn their keep. Win the group's beach club or day club — the furniture, the zones, the dayparts, the packages — on Clubtech.

Because Clubtech sits on top of your existing POS and PMS rather than replacing them — Opera PMS, Airwallex, Midtrans, Apple Pay, Google Pay, WhatsApp — it slots alongside a bar-and-club stack with no major IT changes, and the guest never sees a seam. FINNS Beach Club in Bali runs the furniture model at scale; as CEO Beau Whittington puts it, the venue has "grown from simple on-the-day bookings with no financial guarantee… to now having millions of dollars worth of pre-paid bookings each month."

On commercials, the two models differ in kind. Clubtech charges no monthly fee. There's a 4% online processing fee paid by the guest, and a one-time $2,000 setup per venue that covers widget setup, configuration, and staff training. Adding a furniture channel next to an existing bar stack doesn't add another per-site monthly line — book a demo to see it on your own floor plan.

Questions operators ask

What is the best Access Collins alternative for a beach club?

For beach clubs, day clubs, and hotel pools, Clubtech is built around the transaction those venues actually run: guests pick their exact spot on a 3D interactive map, buy tiered packages with stackable add-ons, and prepay, with every booking feeding Meta, Google, and GA4 as a revenue event. Access Collins is a strong UK bar-and-club bookings system, but its enquiry-and-bookings model isn't a furniture map with zone and daypart pricing. If the deck is your product, that's the gap Clubtech closes.

How much does Access Collins cost compared to Clubtech?

Access Collins publishes pricing from £149/month per site, scaling by site type, size, and features. Clubtech has no monthly fee. Instead there's a 4% online processing fee paid by the customer and a one-time $2,000 setup fee per venue that covers widget setup, configuration, and staff training. For a multi-site operator, that's the difference between a recurring per-site subscription and a cost aligned to online revenue.

Is Access Collins a good collins booking system alternative outside the UK?

Collins is UK-centric, and its consumer discovery reach through DesignMyNight is a UK strength. Venues in Southeast Asia, the Mediterranean, or the Gulf usually need Midtrans (QRIS, GoPay, OVO), Airwallex, and multi-currency checkout, which Clubtech supports natively. If your guests pay in local rails and you're selling furniture, a UK-centric bookings module is a harder fit than a platform built for those regions.

What about the DesignMyNight discovery audience?

DesignMyNight's up-to-3.9-million monthly users are a real audience, but the venue reaches it through the DesignMyNight brand — you rent that discovery traffic. Clubtech is a different model: white-label on your own domain, your paid channels, and a server-side ads loop that posts booking revenue back to Meta, Google, and GA4. The trade is discovery reach on someone else's brand versus an owned channel that builds your own remarketing list.

Can I run Access Collins and Clubtech together?

Yes — the practical split for a group is Collins for the UK bars and clubs, Clubtech for the beach club, day club, or pool. Enquiry management, pre-orders, and DesignMyNight discovery stay where they work; the furniture, zones, dayparts, and packages sell on the map. Clubtech sits on top of your existing POS and PMS with no major IT changes, so adding it doesn't mean ripping anything out.

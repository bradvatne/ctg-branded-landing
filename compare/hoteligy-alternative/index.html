The Hoteligy alternative that sells your pool to guests you haven't met yet

Hoteligy digitizes the pool for guests already checked in. Clubtech turns the same deck into an outside-guest revenue channel. Here's the difference.

Book a Demo See the Platform

Your pool deck is the most photographed square meterage on the property — and, for most hotels, the least monetized. Hoteligy and Clubtech both put that deck on a bookable map, which is why they end up in the same evaluation. But they answer different questions. Hoteligy asks: how do guests who already checked in reserve a sunbed? Clubtech asks: how does the deck earn revenue from everyone — residents, outside day guests, and the premium packages both are willing to pay for? If your pool's ceiling is your room count, that's the question worth changing.

What Hoteligy is

Hoteligy is a Spanish hotel-digitization platform: a no-download guest webapp, activated by QR or NFC and hosted on the hotel's own domain, with more than 18 modules covering digital menus, activities, guest requests, and more. It reports 500+ hotels across 26+ countries, with chains including Iberostar, RIU, H10, Radisson Blu, and Sheraton among its users — a genuinely established footprint across the Med and Caribbean resort world.

Its sunbed module deserves specific credit, because it's capable: an interactive pool and beach map where guests pick their own sunbeds, multiple independent zones, lead-time dynamic pricing configurable per zone, upsells like minimum drink orders and premium towels, and a staff panel with auto-release for no-shows. PMS integrations run deep — Oracle Opera, Sihot, SAP, Protel — so guest segments and VIP courtesies flow from the property system. Payments run through Stripe and Redsys; pricing is quote-only.

For an all-inclusive resort GM digitizing in-stay services — where sunbed booking is one convenience among menus, activities, and requests — Hoteligy is a strong, proven suite. This page won't pretend otherwise.

The frame: amenity software vs commerce software

The structural difference isn't a feature; it's who the software is pointed at. Hoteligy's flow serves residents — people the hotel has already acquired through room bookings. The webapp activates on property, the guest reserves a lounger, service improves, complaints drop. That's amenity management, and done well it protects the guest experience.

Clubtech points the same deck outward. Think of pool revenue as three layers:

  1. Resident reservations. Guests in-house reserving loungers — the layer Hoteligy covers well, and Clubtech handles with Opera PMS integration so resident bookings land on the folio.
  2. Outside-guest day passes. The paying public: locals and travelers who will never book a room but will pay for a day on a premium deck. This layer needs a public booking funnel — a storefront a stranger can find, trust, and pay through — not a QR code in the lobby.
  3. Premium package upsell. The margin stack on both audiences: the cabana with a bottle package, the daybed with minimum spend, the transfer, the celebration cake — tiered from entry pass to ultimate experience, sold as one-tap add-ons at checkout.

Hoteligy's revenue depth is layer one plus drink minimums and towel upgrades. Layers two and three are Clubtech's ground: guests open a 3D birds-eye interactive map, explore zones, watch 360° walkthroughs, tap the exact daybed or cabana they want, and climb the package ladder before they pay. That's the difference between a $5 sunbed reservation and a $400 booking on the same square meter of deck.

Acquisition: the layer no amenity suite needs

Here's the honest reason Hoteligy doesn't market an ads pipeline: its audience is already on property. Nothing needs acquiring. The moment you open the deck to outside guests, that logic reverses — every day-pass guest has to be found, converted, and brought back, which makes the marketing plumbing the product.

On Clubtech, the booking is the conversion event. Day-pass and cabana bookings fire to Meta, Google, and GA4 in real time with revenue values posted back, so campaigns optimize on dollars. Abandoned checkouts trigger CAPI events within seconds, and dynamic ads return the guest to the exact zone, date, and price they left. Lookalike audiences are seeded from your highest-value bookers, and the platform captures marketing data on every guest in the party — not just the booker — so the marketable audience compounds with every reservation. When the deck sells out, priority-entry capture turns the miss into a lead.

This also reframes the marketplace question. Hotels exploring day-pass revenue often start with a listing on a marketplace — and pay commission on a guest whose account stays with the platform. An owned channel on your domain keeps the pricing, the margin, and the guest relationship. The full architecture — booking flow, operations, and attribution in one system — is on the platform page.

Where each platform fits

  • Choose Hoteligy if the job is digitizing in-stay guest services at scale — menus, activities, requests, and sunbed reservations for residents, in one webapp, tightly wired to your PMS. All-inclusive resorts with high occupancy and no outside-guest ambitions sit squarely in its lane.
  • Choose Clubtech if the job is making the pool a revenue channel: selling day passes to non-residents, packaging cabanas and daybeds into tiered products, pricing dayparts to demand, and running paid acquisition against all of it. That's the hotel pool booking model — residents and day guests on one map, folio integration included.
  • Meeting both in one hotel is plausible. A resort can run Hoteligy's guest app for in-stay services while Clubtech powers the public-facing pool and beach commerce. Clubtech integrates with the existing PMS and POS rather than replacing them, so the outside-guest channel arrives with no major IT changes.

On commercials: Hoteligy is quote-only, scaled by zones and resources. Clubtech's model is simple and aligned — no monthly fee, and the platform earns when your online revenue grows, so book a demo for a quote built around your venue. Behind the model: founded in Singapore, venue partners in more than 7 countries, $332k in weekly GMV processed, and a named proof point in FINNS Beach Club in Bali, where the CEO describes millions of dollars worth of pre-paid bookings each month.

Questions operators ask

What is Hoteligy?

Hoteligy is a Spanish hotel guest-experience platform: a QR/NFC-activated webapp on the hotel's domain with 18+ modules spanning digital menus, activities, requests, and sunbed booking. It reports 500+ hotels in 26+ countries, integrates with Oracle Opera, Sihot, SAP, and Protel, and takes payments through Stripe and Redsys. Pricing is quote-based, scaled by zones and resources.

Does Hoteligy handle sunbed booking?

Yes, and its sunbed module is genuinely capable: an interactive pool and beach map with guest-selected sunbeds, independent zones, lead-time dynamic pricing, upsells like drink minimums and premium towels, and a staff panel with auto-release. The scope is in-stay: it serves guests already checked in through the hotel webapp, rather than operating a public day-pass storefront for outside guests.

How is Clubtech different from Hoteligy?

Clubtech is venue commerce rather than an in-stay amenity suite. It sells the deck to residents and outside day guests alike — 3D map booking with 360° walkthroughs, tiered cabana and daybed packages with stackable add-ons, prepayment, and dynamic pricing — while every booking fires to Meta, Google, and GA4 with revenue posted back for paid acquisition. Opera PMS integration keeps resident bookings on the folio.

Can hotels sell pool day passes to non-guests?

Yes — day-guest revenue is a fast-growing line for urban and resort hotels, and it doesn't require a marketplace. A white-label engine on the hotel's own domain sells entry passes, daybeds, and cabana packages to outside guests at full margin, with the guest data owned by the hotel. Prepayment and minimum spends protect the deck from no-shows, and residents keep priority through PMS-integrated booking.

Can Hoteligy and Clubtech run in the same hotel?

Yes, because they occupy different layers. Hoteligy can keep running in-stay services — menus, requests, activities — while Clubtech powers the commercial pool and beach operation: outside-guest day passes, premium packages, and the paid-social loop that fills soft days. Clubtech sits on top of the existing PMS and POS stack, so adding the commerce layer doesn't disturb the guest-app layer.

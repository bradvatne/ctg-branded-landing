One live object

The floor plan is the booking map.

Guests book from a 3D birds-eye interactive map of the venue — real zones, exact furniture. Internally, that same map is your floor plan. Not an export of it, not a copy updated overnight: the same object, viewed from the operator’s side.

  • Online sales sync to the internal floor plan in real time
  • A cabana sold at 11:42am is on the floor plan at 11:42am, with the package attached
  • No version drift — no pending edits someone has to carry across
  • Only one list, and it is always current

Most venue management software treats the reservation and the floor as two systems that get reconciled at 6pm, by hand, on a whiteboard. When the thing guests buy is furniture in a zone at a daypart — a daybed until 4pm, a booth from 11 — reconciliation is where the money leaks.

Automatic seating allocation

The floor places itself as bookings land.

The 6pm whiteboard session — a manager, a printout, and forty parties to place before service — is the daily tax venues pay for keeping bookings and the floor in separate systems. Clubtech pays it for you.

  • Confirmed parties are placed onto the live floor plan as bookings land
  • The team reviews a floor that is already placed, instead of building one from scratch
  • Allocation hours go back into service — briefing hosts, walking the zones, working the arrivals
  • The late Friday-night booking for Saturday doesn’t trigger a redo; it simply appears, placed

Real-time inventory sync

One inventory. Zero double-sells.

Double-sells have one cause: two channels selling from two lists. The website sells daybed 14, the host stand sells daybed 14 to a walk-up, and at 1pm two parties are standing over the same furniture while a manager improvises a refund.

  • You set what the team actually sells — furniture, zones, dayparts, and the packages on each
  • Online and walk-up draw down the same pool, synced in real time
  • When a sunbed sells online, it is gone at the host stand in the same moment
  • No buffer stock held back “just in case,” no spreadsheet reconciliation, no apology round at 1pm

  • The levers that grow the number — prepayments, upsells, dynamic pricing — live in the booking journey; the reports that read it live in guest intelligence

FAQ

Questions operators ask.

What software do venues use to manage the floor?

+

Most venues run a patchwork: a reservations tool, a spreadsheet for allocation, a clipboard at the door, and a POS that talks to none of them. Venue operations software like Clubtech replaces the patchwork with one system — the booking map, the floor plan, the guest list, and inventory are the same live object, so what sells online is exactly what the team sees on the floor.

How does automatic seating allocation work?

+

Every confirmed booking is placed onto the live floor plan automatically, so parties have furniture assigned before service starts instead of being dealt out in a pre-shift huddle. The team reviews a placed floor rather than building one from scratch — and because the plan updates as online sales land, the picture stays current right up to doors.

How do venues stop double-booking sunbeds and tables?

+

Run every channel off one inventory. Double-sells happen when online booking and walk-up sales draw from different lists that reconcile too late. On Clubtech, online sales sync to the internal floor plan in real time and the floor team sells from that same live inventory — when a daybed is gone, it is gone everywhere at once.

Can the door team see online bookings in real time?

+

Yes. Online bookings, guest-list registrations, and ticket sales land in the same system the door works from, updating in real time. The door team sees confirmed, prepaid parties as they book — not a printout from 6pm — and QR-code check-in confirms tickets at the line without radioing the office.

Does Clubtech replace the guest list clipboard?

+

Yes. Guest-list and visitor management is built into the platform, so the door works from one system instead of a clipboard plus promoters' phones. Free-entry registrations capture contact details as guests sign up, which means the list finally builds a marketable database instead of evaporating at close.

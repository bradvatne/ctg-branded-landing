Your pool deck is the most photographed square meterage on the property — and, for most hotels, the least monetized. Hoteligy and Clubtech both put that deck on a bookable map, which is why they end up in the same evaluation. But they answer different questions. Hoteligy asks: how do guests who already checked in reserve a sunbed? Clubtech asks: how does the deck earn revenue from everyone — residents, outside day guests, and the premium packages both are willing to pay for? If your pool's ceiling is your room count, that's the question worth changing.

## What Hoteligy is

Hoteligy is a Spanish hotel-digitization platform: a no-download guest webapp, activated by QR or NFC and hosted on the hotel's own domain, with more than 18 modules covering digital menus, activities, guest requests, and more. It reports 500+ hotels across 26+ countries, with chains including Iberostar, RIU, H10, Radisson Blu, and Sheraton among its users — a genuinely established footprint across the Med and Caribbean resort world.

Its sunbed module deserves specific credit, because it's capable: an interactive pool and beach map where guests pick their own sunbeds, multiple independent zones, lead-time dynamic pricing configurable per zone, upsells like minimum drink orders and premium towels, and a staff panel with auto-release for no-shows. PMS integrations run deep — Oracle Opera, Sihot, SAP, Protel — so guest segments and VIP courtesies flow from the property system. Payments run through Stripe and Redsys; pricing is quote-only.

For an all-inclusive resort GM digitizing in-stay services — where sunbed booking is one convenience among menus, activities, and requests — Hoteligy is a strong, proven suite. This page won't pretend otherwise.

## The frame: amenity software vs commerce software

The structural difference isn't a feature; it's who the software is pointed at. Hoteligy's flow serves residents — people the hotel has already acquired through room bookings. The webapp activates on property, the guest reserves a lounger, service improves, complaints drop. That's amenity management, and done well it protects the guest experience.

Clubtech points the same deck outward. Think of pool revenue as three layers:

1. **Resident reservations.** Guests in-house reserving loungers — the layer Hoteligy covers well, and Clubtech handles with Opera PMS integration so resident bookings land on the folio.
2. **Outside-guest day passes.** The paying public: locals and travelers who will never book a room but will pay for a day on a premium deck. This layer needs a public booking funnel — a storefront a stranger can find, trust, and pay through — not a QR code in the lobby.
3. **Premium package upsell.** The margin stack on both audiences: the cabana with a bottle package, the daybed with minimum spend, the transfer, the celebration cake — tiered from entry pass to ultimate experience, sold as one-tap add-ons at checkout.

Hoteligy's revenue depth is layer one plus drink minimums and towel upgrades. Layers two and three are Clubtech's ground: guests open a 3D birds-eye interactive map, explore zones, watch 360° walkthroughs, tap the exact daybed or cabana they want, and climb the package ladder before they pay. That's the difference between a $5 sunbed reservation and a $400 booking on the same square meter of deck.

Here's the honest reason Hoteligy doesn't market an ads pipeline: its audience is already on property. Nothing needs acquiring. The moment you open the deck to outside guests, that logic reverses — every day-pass guest has to be found, converted, and brought back, which makes the marketing plumbing the product.

Lookalike audiences are seeded from your highest-value bookers, and the platform captures marketing data on every guest in the party — not just the booker — so the marketable audience compounds with every reservation. When the deck sells out, priority-entry capture turns the miss into a lead.

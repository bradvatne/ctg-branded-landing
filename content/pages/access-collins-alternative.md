Access Collins and Clubtech both take venue bookings. The useful distinction is what the guest is buying. A restaurant or bar usually starts with party size, date, and time. A beach club, day club, or hotel pool often sells a specific daybed, cabana, zone, package, and daypart.

That difference should drive the shortlist.

## Best fit for Access Collins

Access Collins is a strong fit for hospitality teams that need to manage bookings and enquiries, allocate tables or areas, take pre-orders and payments, and run the day from a diary or floorplan. Its current help center documents auto-confirmation rules, booking types, minimum spends, payments, booking reports, and front-of-house workflows.

That makes it a defensible choice for restaurants, bars, pubs, and multi-site hospitality groups whose main job is controlling covers and enquiries. It should not be dismissed as a simple form or lead inbox.

## Clubtech vs Access Collins at a glance

| Decision area | Clubtech | Access Collins |
|---|---|---|
| Primary transaction | Exact furniture, zones, packages, and dayparts | Bookings and enquiries by party, date, time, table, or area |
| Guest experience | 3D birds-eye map and 360° walkthroughs | Booking flow configured around venue booking types |
| Revenue setup | Prepayments, deposits, minimum spends, packages, add-ons, and dynamic pricing | Payments, pre-orders, minimum spends, offers, and booking rules |
| Service day | Online inventory sync, internal floor plan, allocation, and operator console | Diary, floorplan, run sheets, check-in, and booking management |
| Marketing loop | Booking values sent to Meta, Google, and GA4; abandoned-booking events | Reporting and guest data inside the Access booking workflow |
| Commercials | Scoped to the venue and confirmed after discovery | Confirm current Access pricing directly with the vendor |

## Where Clubtech fits

Clubtech fits when the venue's physical inventory is the storefront. Guests see the deck, compare zones, choose the exact furniture they want, and add a package before payment. The same reservation then reaches the floor team through a live operator map.

The difference is not that Access Collins lacks operations or payment tools. It is that Clubtech starts with spatial, guest-facing furniture selection and carries that choice through pricing, allocation, guest data, and attribution. See the full workflow on the [Clubtech platform](/platform/#booking) and the venue-specific version on the [beach club solution](/solutions/beach-clubs/).

## Coexistence and switching considerations

A group does not need one answer for every concept. Access Collins can remain the booking system for restaurants and bars while Clubtech handles a beach club, day club, or pool where exact furniture and dayparts shape the sale.

If you are considering a switch, ask both vendors to show the same Saturday from start to finish:

1. A guest selects a premium zone and exact piece of furniture.
2. The guest adds a package and pays a deposit or minimum spend.
3. Front of house sees the reservation and allocation on the service-day floor.
4. The booking value reaches the venue's analytics and advertising accounts.

The point is not to count features. It is to see which workflow matches the inventory you sell.

## Questions operators ask

### Is Access Collins only an enquiry tool?

No. Its current official documentation covers bookings, floorplans, table and area allocation, pre-orders, payments, minimum spends, check-in, and reporting. The fair comparison is between two different booking models, not between a complete product and a form.

### When is Clubtech the better fit?

Clubtech is the better fit when guests need to choose exact furniture or zones, buy tiered packages and add-ons, prepay, and carry that spatial choice into service-day allocation.

### Can Access Collins and Clubtech run in the same group?

Yes. A group can keep Access Collins for restaurant or bar concepts and use Clubtech for a furniture-led beach club, day club, nightclub, or hotel pool.

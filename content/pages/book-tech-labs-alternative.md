Book Tech Labs and Clubtech begin from the same useful premise: a beach-club guest should be able to choose the furniture they want visually. This is not a covers-based system versus a map. It is a comparison between two products built around bookable physical inventory.

The evaluation therefore needs to move beyond the first screen.

## Best fit for Book Tech Labs

Book Tech Labs publicly positions its BOOK Beach Club product as an interactive booking engine for beach clubs, with visual selection of sunbeds and cabanas. Its broader product positioning also covers clubs, resorts, marinas, attractions, and F&B venues.

It belongs on a serious shortlist when the priority is a visual guest booking flow across several experience categories. Product depth, implementation, integrations, and current commercials should be confirmed in a live vendor demonstration.

## Clubtech vs Book Tech Labs at a glance

| Decision area | Clubtech | Book Tech Labs |
|---|---|---|
| Guest selection | 3D birds-eye map, exact furniture, zones, and 360° walkthroughs | Interactive visual selection for beach-club inventory |
| Revenue setup | Tiered packages, stackable add-ons, prepayments, deposits, and dynamic pricing | Confirm package, add-on, and pricing depth in the current product demo |
| Service day | Live inventory sync, internal floor plan, automatic allocation, and operator console | Ask to see the operational console behind the guest flow |
| Marketing loop | Booking revenue to Meta, Google, and GA4; abandoned-booking events within seconds | Confirm current server-side attribution and recovery workflow |
| Delivery | Setup includes widget configuration and staff training | Confirm implementation scope, data migration, and training |
| Commercials | Scoped to the venue and confirmed after discovery | Confirm directly with Book Tech Labs |

## Where Clubtech fits

Clubtech is strongest when the operator wants one chain from the guest's furniture choice to the floor team and the marketing account. The guest picks the spot, adds a package, prepays, and the booking updates the live operating map. Revenue values are also posted to Meta, Google, and GA4, while abandoned-booking events retain the exact zone, date, and price.

Those mechanics are explained on the [booking platform](/platform/#booking), the [front-of-house operations section](/platform/#operations), and the [attribution workflow](/grow/#ads).

## Coexistence and switching considerations

Because both products are map-first, do not decide from a homepage or a polished picker. Use one venue configuration and ask each team to demonstrate:

1. How the same daybed changes price by zone, date, demand, and daypart.
2. How packages and add-ons are configured and reported.
3. What the floor team sees before doors open.
4. Which conversion and revenue events reach Meta, Google, and GA4 without agency work.
5. What implementation, training, and migration require from the venue team.

Coexistence may make sense during a staged migration, but running two guest booking engines for the same inventory creates avoidable reconciliation risk. Set one source of truth for each inventory pool.

## Questions operators ask

### Do both products support visual furniture booking?

Yes. Book Tech Labs publicly markets visual sunbed and cabana selection, while Clubtech uses a 3D birds-eye map with exact-furniture selection and 360° walkthroughs.

### What should decide the evaluation?

Pricing depth, service-day operations, attribution, implementation, and the total commercial model. The guest picker is only the first part of the system.

### Is this a switch or coexistence decision?

For the same inventory it is usually a switch decision, because two engines create availability and reporting conflicts. A staged rollout can still reduce migration risk.

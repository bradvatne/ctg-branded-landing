ResortPass and Clubtech solve different parts of the hotel day-guest problem. ResortPass creates demand through a consumer marketplace for pools, cabanas, spas, day rooms, and other amenities. Clubtech gives the property a white-label direct channel for exact pool furniture, packages, payment, floor operations, and guest data.

The strategic question is channel mix, not whether one model is universally better.

## Best fit for ResortPass

ResortPass is a strong fit when a hotel wants access to an established day-guest marketplace. Its current partner materials position the product around incremental demand for hotels, resorts, and spas, with inventory, pricing, reservations, reporting, and partner support tools.

That discovery can be valuable for a property entering the day-pass category or filling periods where direct demand is weak. Marketplace terms and fees are property-specific and should be confirmed in the current partner agreement.

## Clubtech vs ResortPass at a glance

| Decision area | Clubtech | ResortPass |
|---|---|---|
| Channel model | Direct, white-label booking on the property's domain | Consumer day-guest marketplace |
| Demand | Hotel's brand, website, CRM, and paid channels | ResortPass marketplace audience and marketing |
| Inventory | Exact daybeds, cabanas, tables, zones, and dayparts | Day passes, cabanas, daybeds, spa products, day rooms, and packages listed by the property |
| Revenue controls | Tiered packages, stackable add-ons, prepayments, deposits, minimum spends, and dynamic pricing | Property-managed product inventory and pricing in the partner portal |
| Guest data | Venue-owned; every invited guest can supply their own details | Marketplace booking and partner reservation data; confirm permitted use in the agreement |
| Commercials | Scoped to the venue and confirmed after discovery | Confirm commission, processing, and agreement terms with ResortPass |

## Where Clubtech fits

Clubtech fits when the hotel wants its own day-guest channel. A guest books on the hotel domain, selects the exact cabana or daybed, chooses a package, prepays, and arrives against inventory already visible to the operating team. Booking values can feed Meta, Google, and GA4, so direct acquisition is measured against revenue.

## Coexistence and switching considerations

A marketplace and a direct channel can coexist. Use ResortPass where its audience adds demand, and direct repeat visitors or brand-led demand to the hotel's own channel. Avoid offering the same unit twice unless inventory is synchronized or deliberately allocated by channel.

Before reducing marketplace inventory, measure net revenue, acquisition source, repeat behavior, package attachment, and operational workload. The right sequence is often to build the direct channel first, then adjust marketplace allocation with evidence.

## Questions operators ask

### Is ResortPass a booking engine or a marketplace?

Its current official partner site describes ResortPass as a day-guest marketplace. It also provides partner tools for pricing, inventory, reservations, and reporting.

### Does a direct channel mean leaving ResortPass?

No. A hotel can use ResortPass for incremental discovery and Clubtech for direct, brand-owned demand. The inventory boundary must be explicit.

### Who owns the guest relationship?

Clubtech runs on the venue's domain with venue-owned guest data. For ResortPass bookings, review the current partner agreement and privacy terms to understand which guest fields the property receives and how they may be used.

In Dubai, the platform you're most likely replacing — or sitting next to — isn't a Silicon Valley name. It's serVme. Built in the region, priced without per-cover commissions, and installed at some of the city's best-known venues, it has earned its place at the host stand. The question for a beach club or day club operator is narrower: serVme manages your floor. Who sells your furniture?

## What serVme is

serVme is a reservation, guest CRM, and F&B management platform founded in 2017 and headquartered in Dubai, with roots in Beirut and an expanding European footprint. It reports more than 3,000 venues across MENA, serving restaurants, hotel F&B groups, enterprise operators, and nightlife concepts. The regional depth is real: WhatsApp reminders and no-show prevention built in, payment partnerships covering Saudi, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Jordan, and Egypt, two-way POS sync through partners like Foodics, and a client list that includes a deep Hyatt roster alongside beach names like Cove Beach and Yas Beach.

Its commercial stance is a genuine strength too: a commission-free flat subscription rather than per-cover fees, with deposits, pre-authorizations, and no-show rules configurable per shift, event, or party size, plus event ticketing through integrated payments.

If you run restaurant covers in the Gulf — a dining room, a hotel F&B group, a brunch program — serVme is a credible, regionally fluent choice, and this page won't argue otherwise.

## The mismatch: managing the floor vs selling it

Here's the distinction that decides the fit. serVme's floor plan is an operator-facing tool: staff see the room on an iPad, drag reservations onto tables, and run service. That's table management, and serVme does it well.

A beach club's revenue problem is different. The guest — not the host — needs to see the floor, because the floor is the product:

- **Guests don't book a table; they buy a spot.** A front-row daybed and a back-row daybed both "seat four," but they're different products at different prices. On serVme, a guest books a reservation for six at 1pm; on Clubtech, they open a 3D birds-eye map, explore zones, watch 360° walkthroughs, and tap the exact bed they want — commitment happens before the price appears.
- **The package ladder lives at checkout.** Bed only, bed with minimum spend, party package, ultimate experience — with bottles, cakes, and transfers stacked on as one-tap add-ons. On a covers-and-tickets model, that ladder gets approximated with notes and upsell lists instead of sold as first-class inventory.
- **Dayparts and dynamic pricing.** Morning and afternoon sessions on the same furniture, sunset premiums, event-day rates. Clubtech prices the same bed to demand so the deck earns more without adding a single lounger.
- **The storefront itself.** serVme reaches guests through widget-based booking embeds; Clubtech runs a full white-label storefront — your domain, your design, your guest data — plus day-pass sales for guests who want entry before they want furniture.

None of this is a knock on serVme's engineering. It's a different unit of sale. Guest management organizes demand that already arrived; furniture commerce manufactures higher-value demand before the weekend.

## The ads loop: where the budgets actually go

Dubai beach clubs fill Saturdays with paid social, and this is where the platform gap widens. On Clubtech, the booking is the conversion event: every paid reservation fires to Meta, Google, and GA4 in real time with its revenue value posted back, abandoned checkouts trigger CAPI events within seconds so dynamic ads return the guest to the exact zone, date, and price they left, and lookalike audiences are seeded from your highest-LTV bookers rather than follower counts.

serVme's marketing tooling is CRM-led — guest profiles, automated follow-ups, WhatsApp campaigns — which is exactly right for driving restaurant repeat visits. What its public materials don't describe is that server-side ads pipeline. If paid acquisition is your growth engine, put the question to any vendor directly: does a paid booking, with its revenue value, reach the ad platform automatically — and does an abandoned cart come back as a retargetable event?

There's a capture difference on the venue side too: Clubtech records marketing data on every guest in the party, not just the booker, and when furniture sells out, priority-entry capture turns "sold out" into a lead instead of a bounce. Booking patterns — revenue by hour, lead time, product mix, origin markets — land in 20+ reports the venue owns.

Going live on Clubtech is a five-stage implementation run by a dedicated account lead: onboarding, where we scope the venue, inventory, systems, and commercial model; build; training; go-live; and a 90-day optimize phase. Most operators aren't afraid of new software. They're afraid of the six weeks in the middle — the half-migrated inventory, the floor team running two systems on a sold-out Saturday, the IT project that quietly becomes their job. This page walks through how a Clubtech rollout actually runs, stage by stage, and why switching costs less than staying put.

Stage 1 — Onboarding: scope the venue before configuring anything

Every rollout starts with your account lead learning how the venue makes money — before a single screen is configured. Not a questionnaire: a working session on the commercial model. What do you actually sell? Daybeds and cabanas by daypart, VIP tables with minimum spends, event tickets, day passes? Where do the minimums sit, what gets bundled, what should cost more on a Saturday than a Tuesday?

The same session maps the inventory — every zone and piece of furniture that will appear on the booking map — and the systems the venue already runs: POS, PMS, payment rails. Clubtech is configured on top of that stack, so onboarding is where we confirm what stays exactly as it is (usually: everything).

This is the stage that makes "no one-size-fits-all package" more than a slogan. A generic setup forces the venue to sell the software's model. Scoping first means the platform is shaped around yours.

Stage 2 — Build: configure the journey around what you actually sell

With the scope agreed, your lead configures the booking journey. The 3D birds-eye booking map is built for your actual floor — the real zones, the real furniture, 360° walkthroughs of the spots guests are choosing between. Packages are laddered the way you priced them in onboarding: bed only, party package, ultimate, with stackable add-ons like bottles, cakes, and transfers.

The revenue mechanics get set here too: dynamic pricing rules so the same daybed prices to demand, "Book Online & Save" spreads that reward early commitment, and events and ticketing with QR check-in where the calendar calls for it. Because the widget is white-label, all of it ships on your domain, in your design, feeding your guest database.

A beach club, a nightclub, and a hotel pool don't sell the same way, and the build reflects that — see the solutions by venue type for how the journey differs by concept.

Stage 3 — Training: every team that touches a booking

Training covers the four teams a booking passes through, and it's included in the setup fee — not sold back to you as a services line.

  • Reservations — managing bookings, packages, and prepayments through the operator console.
  • Floor — the interactive internal floor plan: automatic seating allocation, real-time inventory sync, reading the service day before doors open.
  • Door — guest-list and visitor management, QR check-in, priority-entry capture when the venue sells out.
  • Marketing — the ads and reporting workflow: bookings firing to Meta, Google Ads, and GA4 with revenue posted back, and the reports the team will run every week.

The point isn't software fluency for its own sake. It's that on the first live service day, nobody at the venue is meeting the system for the first time.

Stage 4 — Go-live: launched with a lead, not a ticket queue

Launch day is staffed. Your dedicated account lead — the same person who scoped the venue in stage one — is on with real-time support as the first online bookings land and the first prepaid parties hit the floor plan.

That continuity is deliberate. The person watching your go-live knows why the cabana minimums are set where they are and which zones you expect to sell first, because they wrote the scope. There's no handoff to a generic queue at exactly the moment questions get specific.

Stage 5 — Optimize: 90 days of hypercare and data advisory

Go-live isn't the finish line; it's the start of the data. The first 90 days run as hypercare — the account lead stays close while the venue settles into the platform — paired with strategic data advisory to improve conversion.

Advisory means reading the numbers with you: 20+ built-in reports covering revenue by hour, lead time, product mix, origin markets, and repeat share, with no CSV exports required. Which packages convert, where guests abandon, what the online-vs-door spread should be next month — the guest intelligence layer turns the booking log into decisions, and the lead helps you act on them.

This loop is how the flagship result happened. FINNS Beach Club CEO Beau Whittington: "We have grown from simple on-the-day bookings with no financial guarantee… to now having millions of dollars worth of pre-paid bookings each month." Not a launch-day outcome — an operating loop that kept improving after go-live.

Switching cost: what actually changes (and what doesn't)

The reason venues delay replacing a booking system is rarely the new software — it's the imagined IT project around it. On Clubtech, that project mostly doesn't exist. The platform sits on top of your existing POS and PMS rather than replacing them, with no major IT changes: integrations include Opera PMS, Airwallex, Midtrans (QRIS, GoPay, OVO), Apple Pay, Google Pay, Meta Ads, Google Ads, GA4, and WhatsApp, with multi-currency support throughout.

The commercials keep the entry cost low too. There's no monthly fee; online bookings carry a 4% processing fee paid by the customer; and the one-time $2,000 setup per venue covers widget setup, system configuration, and staff training — the build and training stages described above. So the honest switching inventory is short: your team learns one new console, your website gains a booking widget, and your guests start paying before they arrive. Your POS, your PMS, and your service flow stay put.

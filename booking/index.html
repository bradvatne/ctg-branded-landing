The 3D interactive booking map

Most booking software sells a category. “Daybed — $150” is a row in a grid, and a row in a grid gets compared on price, because price is the only information on it. The Clubtech booking map sells the venue instead: guests open a 3D birds-eye view of the property, drift over the zones — front row on the sand, swim-up, the shaded terrace, the booth beside the DJ — and tap the specific piece of furniture they want.

From there, 360° walkthroughs put them in the spot: the view from that daybed, the pool from that cabana. The guest picks where their day happens before a price ever appears — emotional commitment first, price reveal second. By checkout they are not evaluating “a sunbed”; they are defending a decision they have already made. That sequencing is the signature difference between Clubtech and grid-first booking tools, and it is why the map is the first thing sales shows a prospect.

The same map sells whatever the venue sells: daybeds and cabanas for a beach club, VIP booths and bottle service for a nightclub floor. And it does not stop at the sale — the map the guest booked on syncs to the internal floor plan the team runs service from, so the front of house sees every prepaid spot before doors open.

Tiered packages: bed only to ultimate

Every zone on the map carries a pricing ladder, not a single number: bed only, the party package, the ultimate experience. The guest who came for a $150 daybed sees, in the same view, what $400 turns the day into — and a meaningful share climbs, because the ladder is presented at the moment of highest intent, not pitched by a server at 2pm.

Anchoring the ladder to the zone is the point. Front row carries a front-row minimum; the perimeter sits below it; each tier is priced against its neighbors. Guests compare your options against each other instead of comparing your venue against the one down the beach. The operator sets the rungs; the map does the selling.

Upsells and add-ons, stacked in the flow

Once the spot is chosen, add-ons stack onto the reservation in the same flow: bottles, celebration cakes, airport or hotel transfers, retail products. One tap each, attached to the booking, paid with it. No upsell conversation on the floor, no “ask your server” — the guest builds the day themselves, and spends more doing it than they would answer to a pitch.

Because this happens online, it happens around the clock. A birthday group planning at midnight on Tuesday adds the cake and the second round of bottles while the venue is dark. That is revenue on top of the reservation, collected 24/7, arriving before the guest does — and it is the cheapest revenue a venue earns, because the acquisition cost was already paid when the booking landed.

Book online and save

A spread between the online price and the walk-up price gives guests a reason to commit early — and early commitment is worth paying for. A booking made on Wednesday is revenue banked, furniture allocated, and a party of six the kitchen and floor can plan around. A walk-up on Saturday is a hope.

Run across the calendar, Book Online & Save converts demand you cannot see into capacity data you can: which dates are filling, which zones are gone, where the soft spots are while there is still time to push them. You read Saturday on Wednesday, and staff, stock, and promote against numbers instead of weather-and-instinct.

Dynamic pricing

The same daybed is not worth the same amount on a sold-out Saturday and a slow Tuesday morning — but on most venues’ rate cards it costs the same anyway, which means peak days are underpriced and quiet dayparts do not clear. Clubtech prices the same furniture to demand, daypart, and remaining inventory, flexing rates automatically inside rules the operator sets.

The result is yield, not gouging: peak inventory captures what it is actually worth, off-peak inventory finds the price at which it fills, and the venue makes more from the same seats without adding one of them. The mechanics — and where operators get it wrong — are covered in depth in dynamic pricing for beach clubs.

Events and ticketing

Full-moon party, headline DJ, New Year’s Eve — event nights run through the same platform, not a third-party ticketing site that keeps the buyer data. A native events widget sells tiered tickets under the venue’s own brand, and QR-code check-in moves the line at the door.

Because tickets and furniture live in one system, they sell together: the guest buying entry sees the table upgrade; the group booking the booth adds tickets for the friends outside it. And every ticket buyer lands in the same guest database as the bookers — an event crowd the venue can market to all season instead of an anonymous gate count.

Priority entry: sold out is not a lost guest

The most expensive moment in the funnel is the one where a guest with money in hand meets a sold-out date and leaves without a trace. Priority-entry capture closes that leak: when furniture sells out, the flow keeps working, taking the guest’s details for priority entry instead of showing a dead end.

The demand that overflowed your best Saturday is the easiest audience you will ever market to — these are people who tried to give you money. Next week’s soft Thursday, the next event on sale, the cancellation that opens up a cabana: sell-out capture turns each of them into a message to a list that already said yes once.

Every guest in the database, not just the booker

A party of eight usually enters a venue’s records as one name and seven strangers. On Clubtech, the booker invites the group and every guest supplies their own details before arrival — so the whole party enters the database, and the marketable audience grows with every reservation at the size of the party, not the size of the booking log.

That data compounds downstream: bookings fire to Meta, Google Ads, and GA4 in real time with revenue attached, and 20+ built-in reports turn the reservation log into guest intelligence — revenue by hour, lead time, product mix, origin markets, repeat share — without a CSV export. The booking journey is where all of it begins.

Gift cards

Venue-branded gift cards, sold online and redeemable against bookings and packages — another way revenue arrives before the visit, and another guest who enters the database before they ever reach the door.

Four-tap checkout, built for the phone

On Clubtech, 82% of bookings happen on a phone after 10pm — the group chat hour, when the plan gets made and whoever checks out first decides where everyone goes. The journey is built for exactly that moment: sub-second load, no app install, Apple Pay and Google Pay, a confirmed prepaid reservation in four taps.

Every extra field and every extra second at that hour is a group lost to a venue with a shorter form — or to “let’s just walk in somewhere.” Four taps is not a vanity metric; it is the difference between capturing the decision at the moment it is made and hoping it survives until morning.

Questions operators ask

How does an online booking system for a beach club work?

Guests open the venue’s own website, pick an exact daybed, cabana, or table on an interactive map, choose a package tier, stack add-ons like bottles or transfers, and prepay. The venue banks the revenue days ahead, the reservation syncs to the floor plan in real time, and every guest in the party enters the marketing database before arrival.

What is an interactive venue booking map?

It is a live model of the venue guests book from directly. On Clubtech it is a 3D birds-eye map: guests explore zones from overhead, tap the specific piece of furniture they want, and watch 360° walkthroughs of the spot before the price appears. They commit to the experience first, then pay for it — instead of choosing a row from a grid.

How do venues sell add-ons online?

Inside the booking flow, as stackable one-tap options attached to the reservation — bottles, celebration cakes, transfers, products. Because the guest has already committed to the spot, attaching spend takes seconds, and the add-on store sells 24/7 whether or not the venue is open. It is revenue on top of the reservation, collected before the guest arrives.

What is dynamic pricing for venues?

Pricing the same furniture differently as demand, daypart, and remaining inventory move — the way airlines price seats. A front-row daybed on a sold-out Saturday should not cost what it costs on a quiet Tuesday. Clubtech flexes rates automatically within rules the operator sets, so peak days yield more and slow days still fill.

Can guests book a beach club or nightclub on mobile?

They already do — on Clubtech, 82% of bookings happen on a phone after 10pm. The checkout is built for that moment: sub-second load, no app install, Apple Pay and Google Pay, and a confirmed, prepaid reservation in four taps. If checkout needs a laptop, the venue is losing the guests who decide in the group chat.

Can a venue booking system sell event tickets too?

Yes. Clubtech includes a native events booking widget alongside furniture reservations, with tiered tickets and QR-code check-in at the door. Ticket buyers land in the same guest database as daybed and table bookers, so an event crowd becomes an audience the venue can market the rest of the season to.
